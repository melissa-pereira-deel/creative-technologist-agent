At any moment, only certain things are possible — the ones adjacent to what already exists. The first cell couldn't become a mammal directly. Git couldn't have existed without distributed computing. The smartphone camera couldn't have replaced the point-and-shoot before cheap CMOS sensors, before cloud storage, before fast mobile networks. Each step in the adjacent possible opens new adjacent possibilities that weren't accessible before.

The concept comes from theoretical biologist Stuart Kauffman and was developed as a theory of innovation by Steven Johnson in *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010). The insight is structural: it explains why good ideas often arrive in clusters — multiple people arriving at the same adjacent possible from different directions — and why some ideas that seem visionary fail not because they're wrong but because they arrive before the adjacent conditions are in place.

For the creative technologist, reading the adjacent possible is a core skill. The question is never just "is this idea good?" but "are all the conditions for this idea already in place?" A product that is one step ahead of the adjacent possible will struggle; a product that reads it correctly will feel inevitable in retrospect.

## Where it shows up

- **Product timing decisions:** is the market, technology, and user behavior ready for this?
- **Feature sequencing:** what must exist before this feature can work?
- **Platform strategy:** which adjacencies are you opening by building this?
- **Competitive analysis:** what will become possible for competitors after you ship this?
