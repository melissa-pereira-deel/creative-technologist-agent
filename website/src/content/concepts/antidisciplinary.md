Interdisciplinary means working across two or more established fields while acknowledging their boundaries — a biologist collaborating with a computer scientist, each bringing their methods to a shared problem. The boundaries remain. The disciplines recognize each other.

Antidisciplinary means something different: occupying a space that no existing discipline has named or claimed. The problems that live there can't be solved by combining two disciplines because neither has the right vocabulary. New tools, new methods, and often entirely new fields have to be invented.

Neri Oxman coined the term to describe her lab's work at the intersection of computation, fabrication, and biology. Her Krebs Cycle of Creativity maps the continuous loop: science encodes, engineering applies, design expresses, art questions. Moving around the cycle produces hybrids that none of the parent disciplines could reach alone — not by combining them but by inhabiting the transitions between them.

The creative technologist occupies an antidisciplinary space: technical depth, behavioral understanding, and aesthetic judgment rarely coexist in a single person or a single discipline. When they do, the work that results feels impossible to categorize and impossible to replicate with specialists working in parallel. The antidisciplinary space is where the most novel problems get solved.

## Where it shows up

- **Team composition:** the creative technologist role itself is antidisciplinary
- **Problem framing:** some problems look unsolvable from inside any single discipline
- **Technology application:** applying methods from biology to software, economics to UX, architecture to information design
- **Career development:** the antidisciplinary practitioner accumulates depth in multiple domains rather than optimizing for a single credential

## The distinction that matters

Interdisciplinary: "We have a biologist and a designer on the team."
Antidisciplinary: "We're working in a space that isn't biology and isn't design — it's something that doesn't have a name yet."
