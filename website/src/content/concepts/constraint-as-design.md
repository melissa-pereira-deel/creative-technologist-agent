A blank canvas is harder than a constrained brief. An infinite budget produces worse results than a tight one. Removing all rules from a game doesn't create freedom — it destroys the game. Constraints don't limit what's possible; they determine what's possible, and in doing so, they create form.

Josh Wardle's Wordle limited players to one puzzle per day. This scarcity — impossible to engineer on digital platforms that optimize for engagement — created anticipation, ritual, and social meaning. Six seconds of video forced Vine creators to invent new forms of compressed narrative. 140 characters created a new form of writing that the unlimited version never could have produced.

John Carmack built Doom on hardware that couldn't render true 3D by inventing rendering techniques that simulated depth so convincingly that the limitation became invisible. His constraints were the design — the specific techniques he invented to work within them are the reason Doom looks like Doom and not like a generic 3D game.

The creative technologist's relationship with constraints inverts the conventional one. The question isn't "how do we work around this?" but "what does this constraint make possible that freedom wouldn't?" This is a shift from treating constraints as problems to treating them as generative material — the specific shape of a constraint determines the specific shape of the solution it makes possible.

## Where it shows up

- **Technical constraints:** hardware limitations, bandwidth limits, latency floors
- **Resource constraints:** small teams, tight deadlines, limited budgets
- **Format constraints:** character limits, time limits, size limits
- **Regulatory constraints:** compliance requirements that force better architecture

## The key move

Name the constraint precisely, then ask: *what does this specific constraint make possible that no constraint wouldn't?* The answer is often the product.
