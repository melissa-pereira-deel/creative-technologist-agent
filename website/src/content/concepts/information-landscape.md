Information doesn't have to be presented linearly — as a sequence, a list, a page to be scrolled. It can have geography: depth, proximity, topology. Closely related ideas can be near each other. Detail can be revealed by zooming in. The structure of the information can encode meaning that no individual data point could carry.

Muriel Cooper arrived at this idea through a career spent thinking about typography as a spatial medium. In 1994, she demonstrated "Information Landscapes" at TED — a three-dimensional space of navigable typography where semantic relationships were expressed through spatial relationships. You could fly through meaning. The concept was a direct ancestor of every zoomable canvas interface (Figma, Miro, Infinite Painter), every spatial computing application, every system that uses layout to communicate structure rather than just organizing content for legibility.

The implication for design is that choosing a representation is choosing what thoughts are possible. A table makes comparison easy. A scatter plot makes correlations visible. A timeline makes sequences apparent. A map makes spatial relationships navigable. The wrong representation doesn't just make information harder to find — it makes certain insights structurally inaccessible, regardless of how much data is present.

The creative technologist asks: *what does this representation make thinkable?*

## Where it shows up

- **Dashboard design:** which representation reveals the insight the decision requires?
- **Data visualization:** the choice of chart type is a design decision about what to make visible
- **Content architecture:** proximity, hierarchy, and grouping encode meaning about relationships
- **Spatial interfaces:** Figma, Miro, and AR/VR environments treat information as navigable space

## The underlying question

Not "how do we display this data?" but "what geography of this information makes the right insights visible at the right resolution?"
