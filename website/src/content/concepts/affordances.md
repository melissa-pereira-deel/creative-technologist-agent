An affordance is a possible action — perceived or actual — that an environment or object offers to an actor. The term comes from ecological psychologist James Gibson, who used it to describe how animals perceive their environment not as a collection of objects with fixed properties but as a field of action possibilities. A flat surface affords standing. A cliff edge affords falling. A branch affords swinging, if you're the right kind of creature.

Don Norman brought the concept into design in *The Design of Everyday Things* (1988), using it to explain why some designed objects are intuitively clear to use and others require manuals. A well-designed affordance makes its possible action immediately perceivable: a handle affords pulling; a flat plate affords pushing.

But the creative technologist's relationship with affordances goes further. It's not about making existing affordances clear — it's about *seeing affordances that weren't intended.* The headphone jack affords audio output. It also affords, if you're looking at the electrical properties rather than the cultural function, inserting any 3.5mm plug — including a credit card reader. Square's founders saw that affordance. Most people didn't.

This is the characteristic move of the creative technologist: treating the fixed properties of a system as generative rather than limiting. Not "what is this for?" but "what does this make possible?"

## Where it shows up

- **Platform strategy:** what does your infrastructure afford that you haven't built products for yet?
- **Technology adoption:** what new affordances does a new material (BLE, LiDAR, on-device ML) open?
- **Competitive analysis:** what affordances is a competitor's product offering that they haven't noticed?
- **Feature design:** what does this feature make possible beyond its stated purpose?

## A note on the origin

James Gibson's concept of affordances was ecological — he was describing perception, not design. Norman's adaptation for design, though influential, changed the meaning in ways Gibson found frustrating. This version is closer to Gibson: affordances as latent possibilities in the structure of a system, discovered rather than designed.
