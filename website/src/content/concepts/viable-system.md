Stafford Beer's Viable System Model (VSM) describes the minimum architecture that any living system — an organism, a company, a city, a product — must have in order to maintain itself, adapt to its environment, and survive over time. It consists of five recursive subsystems: Operations (what the system does), Coordination (managing interactions between operational units), Control (monitoring performance and maintaining stability), Intelligence (scanning the environment and planning for the future), and Policy (defining identity, values, and ultimate purpose).

The model is recursive: each operational unit, looked at closely enough, contains its own viable system structure. A company contains divisions, each of which contains teams, each of which contains individuals — and each level needs the same five subsystems functioning to remain viable.

The key insight is that most product and organizational failures aren't technical failures. They're structural failures: one of the five subsystems is missing, underdeveloped, or disconnected from the others. A product with no Intelligence subsystem (no way to monitor the environment and plan) will be overtaken by events. A product with no Policy subsystem (no clear identity or values) will be pulled in contradictory directions by competing stakeholder demands. A product with no Coordination subsystem will fragment into incoherent parts.

The question "does this work?" is insufficient. The question is "can this system learn?"

## Where it shows up

- **Product architecture:** does the product have mechanisms to observe users, adjust behavior, and improve?
- **Organizational design:** do teams have the five subsystems they need to self-regulate?
- **Platform design:** does the platform create viable sub-systems for its users/operators?
- **Technical architecture:** is the system instrumented to detect and correct its own failures?

## Beer's formulation

"The purpose of a system is what it does." Not what it says it does, not what its designers intended — what it actually produces in the world. This is the most direct test of system viability.
