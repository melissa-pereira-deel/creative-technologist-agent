In 1962, Douglas Engelbart wrote a paper titled "Augmenting Human Intellect: A Conceptual Framework." It asked a question that the entire field of human-computer interaction has been circling ever since: what if computers were designed not to replace human thought but to extend it? Not to automate tasks but to amplify the capacity to reason about complex problems?

Six years later, on December 9, 1968, Engelbart demonstrated what that could look like. In what became known as "the Mother of All Demos," he showed a live audience in San Francisco something that wouldn't become mainstream for another twenty-five years: the mouse, hypertext, collaborative real-time editing, video conferencing, version control. He wasn't demonstrating technology. He was demonstrating a theory of human augmentation.

The distinction Engelbart drew — between automating and augmenting — is the most important design question that most products never ask. Automation saves time; the user remains unchanged. Augmentation changes what the user can think: after using a spreadsheet deeply, you think differently about relationships between numbers. After learning Git, you have a new mental model of branching and history that applies beyond code.

Engelbart spent the rest of his career frustrated that the technology industry had taken his work and turned it into productivity tools — faster horses, not augmented minds. The mouse became a pointer; his dream of bootstrapped collective intelligence remained unrealized. But the question he asked in 1962 remains the highest aspiration for any tool: does it make the person smarter?

## What they gave us

**Amplification vs. Automation** — the distinction between tools that do things for you and tools that make you better at doing things.

## The key idea

*Augmenting human intellect.* The measure of a tool is not how much it saves you from thinking, but how much better it makes your thinking. A tool that makes you dependent is a trap. A tool that makes you capable is a gift.
