Neri Oxman's lab at the MIT Media Lab had a roster that made it impossible to describe in a single sentence: a biomechanical engineer, a glass blower, a computational materials scientist, a marine biologist, an architect, a beekeeper. This was not accidental eclecticism. It was the expression of a theory: that the most significant problems exist in the spaces between established disciplines — the territories no single field claims, and therefore no single field can see clearly.

She called this territory Material Ecology: the idea that computation, fabrication, and biological material are not separate domains to be coordinated but fundamentally continuous. Her lab built structures grown partly by silkworms, printed glass with optical properties that no traditional glassblowing could achieve, and designed biological scaffolds that could degrade organically after serving their purpose. In each case, the question was the same: *what does this material want to do?* — not what can we force it to do.

To map the relationships between disciplines, Oxman developed the Krebs Cycle of Creativity: a diagram showing how science, engineering, design, and art feed into each other in a continuous loop. Science encodes; engineering applies; design expresses; art questions. Move around the cycle and you get hybrids that none of the parent disciplines could produce alone. This isn't interdisciplinary work — working between two established fields while acknowledging both their boundaries. It's antidisciplinary: occupying a space that the existing disciplines can't name, and therefore can't defend.

For the creative technologist, this is the defining condition: to work at the intersection of technical depth, behavioral understanding, and aesthetic craft in a space that no existing job title fully describes.

## Signature moment

In 2013, Oxman's lab released the Silk Pavilion: a structure whose primary architect was a silkworm. The lab created a scaffold using a computer-controlled arm, following the spatial logic of how a silkworm naturally spins. Then they placed 6,500 silkworms on it and let them complete the structure. The worms filled in the gaps, following their own biological imperatives. The resulting dome was partly human, partly algorithmic, partly entirely other.

The project made designers uncomfortable in a specific way: it was not clear who the designer was. The silkworms weren't executing a plan; the computer wasn't imposing a form; Oxman wasn't controlling the outcome. The design emerged from the collaboration between all of them, constrained by material logic and biological behavior. This, Oxman argued, is the right relationship between technology and nature. The designer is not the author. The designer sets the conditions.

## What they gave us

**Antidisciplinary thinking** — the practice of working not between disciplines but in the spaces no discipline claims, where the most novel combinations become possible.

## The key idea

*Nature as the primary client.* When you design for what the material makes possible rather than what the client requests or the discipline permits, you access a different — and often richer — solution space.
