Steven Johnson writes about the environments in which good ideas happen — and, specifically, why good ideas so often arrive in clusters, why the same invention gets invented simultaneously by people who have never met, and why certain historical moments and certain physical spaces seem to generate disproportionate amounts of creative output.

His answer, developed most fully in *Where Good Ideas Come From* (2010), is that innovation is less about individual genius and more about the structure of the possibility space. He borrows the concept of the adjacent possible from the theoretical biologist Stuart Kauffman: at any moment, only certain next steps are possible, given what currently exists. The first cell couldn't become a mammal directly — it had to become a prokaryote, then a eukaryote, then a multicellular organism, each step opening possibilities that the previous step made accessible. The space of what's possible expands only as you explore what's adjacent.

This is why, Johnson argues, the coffeehouse was central to the Enlightenment: it was a place where people from different disciplines brought their partial solutions to shared problems, and the combinations of adjacent possibles multiplied. This is why the coral reef and the city, despite being completely different kinds of systems, share structural properties: both maximize the density of adjacencies, creating the conditions for combinatorial explosion.

For product development, the implication is temporal: the question is never just "is this technically possible?" but "are all the adjacent conditions for this to work also in place?" A product that arrives before its adjacent possible is ready will fail not because it's wrong but because the space around it hasn't yet expanded to include it.

## What they gave us

**The Adjacent Possible** — the concept that innovation is constrained not by imagination but by the set of currently available combinations, and that each new combination expands the space of what can be combined next.

## The key idea

*You can only get to where you're going through the places you can get to.* The art is reading the adjacent possible clearly enough to take the next step at the right time — not too early, not too late.
