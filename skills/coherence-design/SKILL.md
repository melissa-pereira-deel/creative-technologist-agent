# Coherence Design

Christopher Alexander spent decades trying to name a quality he found in buildings and objects that felt "alive" — where nothing is bolted on, every part strengthens the others, and the whole is a different kind of thing entirely. Products have this quality or they don't. You can feel it.

## Model

**Centers Strengthening Centers** — a coherent system is one where each element makes the elements around it MORE alive. A score that makes its dashboard feel more purposeful. A feature that sharpens the product's identity instead of blurring it. Test: does this element strengthen what surrounds it, or does it just exist next to it?

**The Wholeness Test** — in a coherent system, adding or removing something is felt by the whole. In an incoherent system, you can remove features and nobody notices — they were never connected.

**Fitness Between Parts** — not just that each part is good, but that parts fit each other. A brilliant feature in the wrong product is worse than an ordinary feature that belongs.

**Emergent Identity** — coherent products have an identity that emerges from the relationship between parts, not from branding applied on top. Stripe's identity emerges from its API design and documentation quality; Linear's from its speed and keyboard-first design.

**Narrative Coherence** — the product tells a consistent story. Every screen, interaction, and message reinforces the same underlying idea. If one feature undermines the narrative, the whole product feels less coherent even if that feature works in isolation.

## Apply

**Centers Audit** — for each major element (feature, screen, component):
- Does this element strengthen the elements around it?
- Would the product be weaker, the same, or stronger without it?
- Does it reinforce or dilute the product's central identity?
- Can you explain why THIS element belongs in THIS product specifically?

**Narrative Thread** — identify the product's core narrative in one sentence, then trace it through:
- Does onboarding establish it?
- Does each major feature reinforce it?
- Does the empty state communicate it?
- Does error handling respect it?
- Does the pricing model embody it?

**Fitness Test** — when considering a new feature or change:
- Does it fit the product's voice and idiom?
- Would a user who loves the current product welcome this?
- Can you explain it using the product's existing conceptual vocabulary, or does it require new concepts?

**Subtraction Test** — imagine removing this element entirely:
- Would users notice its absence?
- Would other features become less effective?
- Would the product's story become less clear?
- Three "no" answers: the element may not be earning its place.

**Stranger Test** — show the product to someone new. After 2 minutes:
- Can they tell you what it IS?
- Can they tell you what it VALUES?
- Does what they describe match your intention?

## Anti-Patterns

**Feature Parity Without Identity** — copying competitor features without adapting them to your coherence. Result: featureful but identity-less.

**Branding as Substitute** — applying visual consistency on top of structural incoherence. Users see the polish, feel the hollowness.

**Design System Without Design Philosophy** — consistent components that don't add up to a coherent experience.

**"Just Ship It"** — adding features without asking if they strengthen the whole. Coherence debt compounds like technical debt.

**Coherence as Excuse for Stagnation** — real identity is strong enough to expand. "It doesn't fit" is not always the right answer.

## Connections

- **Signal Discrimination** — coherence requires knowing what NOT to add; subtraction preserves coherence
- **Constraint Propagation** — strong product identity IS a constraint that propagates, telling you what fits
- **Trust Architecture** — coherent products are more trustworthy; consistency builds trust
- **Progressive Expertise** — coherence at each expertise level (the product feels whole whether you're a beginner or expert)
